The basics

What is hydroponics?

Hydroponics is a method of growing plants using a water-based nutrient solution instead of soil. The word comes from the Greek hydro (water) and ponos (labor). Rather than letting plants search through soil for nutrients, hydroponics delivers exactly what they need directly to the roots.

Plants don't actually need soil — they need the water, oxygen, minerals, and structural support that soil happens to provide. Hydroponics supplies all of these more directly and efficiently, which is why hydroponic plants often grow significantly faster than their soil counterparts.

The roots are supported by an inert growing medium — rockwool, perlite, clay pebbles, or foam sponges — that holds moisture and oxygen without contributing any nutrients itself. All nutrition comes from a precisely measured liquid solution.

The first large-scale hydroponic systems were developed in the 1930s, but the principles date to the Hanging Gardens of Babylon and ancient Aztec floating garden systems called chinampas. NASA has long studied hydroponics for growing food in space, where soil is impractical.

Today, much of the lettuce, tomatoes, cucumbers, and herbs sold in grocery stores are grown hydroponically in commercial greenhouses. Desktop systems like the iDoo and Suncore bring this same technology to a countertop scale, making it accessible to home growers year-round regardless of climate or outdoor space.

The key variables in any hydroponic system are the same: nutrient concentration, pH, light, temperature, and oxygen at the root zone. Get these right and almost anything will grow.

Benefits

Why grow hydroponically?

Hydroponics offers real, measurable advantages over soil — especially for indoor and year-round growing.

⚡

Faster growth

Plants in hydroponic systems typically grow 30–50% faster than in soil because nutrients are delivered directly to roots with no searching required. Lettuce that takes 60+ days in soil can be ready in 28–35 days hydroponically.

💧

Water efficiency

Closed-loop systems recirculate water continuously. Hydroponic growing uses up to 90% less water than traditional agriculture — the nutrient solution is replenished and reused rather than lost to runoff or evaporation.

🏠

Grow anywhere

No garden, no problem. Desktop systems grow on a countertop, balcony, or shelf. There are no soil pests, no weeding, and no seasonal limitations — you can harvest lettuce in February or tomatoes in December.

🎛️

Precise control

You control exactly what goes into the nutrient solution, the pH, the light cycle, and the growing environment. This makes it easy to dial in optimal conditions for each specific plant and stage of growth.

🌱

Higher yields

Controlled nutrient delivery and optimal lighting mean plants can be grown more densely than in soil. Commercial hydroponic farms regularly achieve yields 3–10× higher per square foot than field agriculture.

🪲

Fewer pests & disease

Soil is the primary vector for many plant pests and diseases. Soilless growing eliminates fungus gnats, nematodes, and soil-borne pathogens. Common problems like root rot can still occur but are far easier to manage.

System types

Types of hydroponic systems

Six main methods, each with different trade-offs for complexity, plant size, and water management. Desktop systems like the iDoo and Suncore are primarily DWC-based.

System How it works Best for Complexity Notes
Deep Water Culture (DWC) Roots suspended in aerated nutrient solution continuously Lettuce, herbs, small fruiting plants Used in most desktop systems including iDoo & Suncore
Nutrient Film Technique (NFT) Thin film of nutrient solution flows continuously over roots in a channel Leafy greens, strawberries Very water-efficient; pump failure is critical
Kratky Method Passive DWC — no pump, air gap between roots and solution Lettuce, herbs Simplest possible system; no electricity needed
Ebb & Flow (Flood & Drain) Grow bed periodically floods with solution then drains back to reservoir Most vegetables, herbs, flowers Versatile; works with many growing media
Drip System Nutrient solution dripped slowly onto base of each plant Tomatoes, peppers, cucumbers Most common in commercial greenhouses
Aeroponics Roots suspended in air and misted with nutrient solution High-value crops, R&D Highest growth rates; used by NASA; complex and costly
What you need

The essentials

Every hydroponic setup — from a Mason jar to a commercial greenhouse — relies on the same core components.

💡 Lighting

  • LED grow lights are the standard — efficient, full-spectrum, and low-heat
  • Leafy greens need 14–16 hours of light per day
  • Fruiting plants need 16–18 hours, especially during vegetative stage
  • Light distance matters — too far causes stretching, too close causes bleaching
  • A timer is essential — consistency is more important than intensity

🌊 Water & reservoir

  • Tap water works for most setups — let it sit 30 min if heavily chlorinated
  • Reservoir should be opaque to prevent algae growth
  • Change the full reservoir every 7–10 days to prevent salt buildup
  • Top off between changes with dilute nutrient solution, not plain water
  • Root zone temperature should stay between 65–72°F

🧪 Nutrients

  • Use a complete hydroponic nutrient solution — soil fertilizers are not adequate
  • Two-part (A+B) solutions prevent nutrients from precipitating out of solution
  • CalMag (calcium/magnesium) supplement is important for most crops
  • FloraBloom (high phosphorus/potassium) boosts flowering and fruiting crops
  • Always add CalMag before A and B when mixing

⚗️ pH & EC meters

  • pH meter is the single most important tool — wrong pH causes nutrient lockout
  • Target pH 5.5–6.5 for most crops (different crops have different preferences)
  • EC meter measures nutrient concentration — prevents over or under-feeding
  • pH tends to drift upward in active systems — check every 1–2 days
  • Store pH probe in storage solution, not plain water — extends probe life

🪨 Growing media

  • Rockwool — most common; excellent water retention and aeration
  • Foam/sponge pods — standard in desktop systems; easy to work with
  • Clay pebbles (LECA) — reusable; great drainage; used in DWC and ebb & flow
  • Perlite — lightweight volcanic glass; good for mixing with other media
  • All media should be pH-neutral and inert — they support roots, not feed them

🌡️ Environment

  • Air temperature: 65–80°F is ideal for most crops
  • Humidity: 50–70% — higher risks mold, lower risks tip burn
  • Air circulation: a small fan improves stem strength and reduces disease
  • CO₂: ambient levels are fine; supplemental CO₂ helps in sealed grow rooms
  • Consistent conditions matter more than perfect conditions
Nutrition & chemistry

Nutrients & pH

Plants need 17 essential elements to grow. Hydroponics delivers all of them through a precisely formulated liquid solution — and pH determines whether plants can actually absorb them.

Essential macronutrients

The six primary nutrients — three mobile (plants can redistribute them) and three secondary

N — Nitrogen
Primary macro
Drives leaf and stem growth. High demand during vegetative stage. Leafy greens need nitrogen-forward nutrition throughout.
P — Phosphorus
Primary macro
Drives root development, flower formation, and fruit set. FloraBloom is phosphorus-rich for this reason.
K — Potassium
Primary macro
Regulates water movement, enzyme activation, and fruit quality. Essential at all stages, higher at fruiting.
Ca — Calcium
Secondary macro
Cell wall strength. Deficiency causes tip burn in lettuce and blossom end rot in tomatoes. Supplied by CALiMAGic.
Mg — Magnesium
Secondary macro
Central atom in chlorophyll. Deficiency shows as yellowing between leaf veins. Also supplied by CALiMAGic.
S — Sulfur
Secondary macro
Protein synthesis and enzyme function. Usually present in sufficient quantities in complete nutrient solutions.

pH targets by crop

Reference

Glossary

Key terms you'll encounter in hydroponic growing.

EC (Electrical Conductivity)
Measures the concentration of dissolved nutrients in your solution. Measured in mS/cm. Higher EC = stronger solution. Low EC causes deficiency; high EC causes nutrient burn.
TDS (Total Dissolved Solids)
Alternative way to measure nutrient concentration, in parts per million (ppm). Often displayed alongside EC on combo meters. Rough conversion: EC × 500 = ppm.
pH (Potential Hydrogen)
Scale from 0–14 measuring acidity/alkalinity. 7.0 is neutral. Most hydroponic crops prefer slightly acidic conditions (5.5–6.5). Controls nutrient availability.
Nutrient lockout
When pH is out of range, nutrients become chemically unavailable even if present. The plant shows deficiency symptoms despite a full reservoir. Fix the pH first before adding more nutrients.
DWC (Deep Water Culture)
Plants roots sit in an oxygenated nutrient solution continuously. The most common method in desktop systems. Requires an air pump or active water circulation for oxygen at the roots.
Root zone
The area immediately surrounding the roots. Temperature, oxygen level, and nutrient availability in the root zone are the primary factors determining plant health and growth rate.
Cotyledons
The first two leaves a seedling produces — round, simple, and generic-looking. These are seed leaves stored in the seed itself, not true leaves. They yellow and fall off naturally as the plant matures.
True leaves
The second pair of leaves that emerge, which look like miniature versions of the adult plant. Their appearance signals the seedling is ready to move into the hydro system and begin thinning.
Bolting
When a leafy plant shifts from vegetative to reproductive mode and sends up a flowering stalk. Lettuce becomes bitter when bolting begins. Triggered by heat, long days, or root stress.
Sucker
A shoot that grows from the junction between a tomato plant's main stem and a leaf branch. If left, it becomes a full stem. In desktop systems, remove suckers to keep 1–2 main stems.
CalMag / CALiMAGic
A calcium and magnesium supplement. Prevents tip burn in lettuce and blossom end rot in tomatoes. Always add to water before Part A and Part B nutrients when mixing.
FloraBloom
A General Hydroponics nutrient formula high in phosphorus and potassium. Introduced at flowering stage in fruiting crops to drive flower and fruit development. Not used for leafy greens.
